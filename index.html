Case Study 01 · Arcolab · 2023–2024
Procure-to-Pay Platform
Digitising enterprise procurement from requisition to payment across 3 organisations
Product Manager · Full Ownership
The Problem
Enterprise procurement at Arcolab's group companies ran on a mix of emails, spreadsheets, and verbal approvals. Purchase requisitions had no central tracking, approvals were bottlenecked on a handful of people, and vendor communication happened across disconnected channels — making audit trails nearly impossible.
My Role
End-to-end product ownership — discovery, roadmap, BRDs, sprint planning, integration coordination, and rollout. I worked directly with procurement heads, finance controllers, and engineering to define requirements and drive delivery.
Discovery & Research

Before writing a single requirement, I spent two weeks conducting discovery interviews with procurement officers, finance controllers, and approval chain managers across three business units. The core insight that shaped everything: the bottleneck wasn't the lack of a system — it was that each organisation had a different approval hierarchy and a different definition of what a "compliant" purchase looked like.

A one-size workflow would never achieve adoption. That single discovery insight drove the most important product decision we made: configurable approval chains, not hardcoded rules.

I also mapped the existing process using service blueprints (built in Figma) to identify where delays clustered — 73% of late approvals were stuck at the second-tier approver, who had no mobile visibility into pending requests.

Key Features Built
Configurable approval chains
Each org defines their own hierarchy — no code change needed
Purchase requisition workflows
Structured intake replacing ad-hoc email requests
PO tracking dashboard
Real-time status for procurement & finance teams
Vendor communication module
Centralised thread replacing scattered email chains
Key Decisions & Tradeoffs
  • Configurable vs. fixed workflows: We scoped a flexible workflow engine instead of hardcoding approval chains. This added ~3 weeks to development but was the single biggest factor in achieving cross-org adoption. A rigid system would have required 3 separate builds.
  • Mobile-first approvals vs. full mobile app: We deprioritised a native mobile app (high effort, low urgency) in favour of a responsive web approval view. This unblocked the key bottleneck — second-tier approvals — with 4x less effort.
  • Vendor portal v1 scope cut: We shipped a lightweight vendor communication module rather than a full self-service vendor portal, accepting that vendors would still need internal liaison support in phase 1. This let us hit the rollout deadline without sacrificing the core procurement flow.
Impact

What I'd do differently
I'd invest more in an NPS or CSAT feedback loop in the first 60 days post-launch. We measured adoption (logins, workflows completed) but didn't systematically capture qualitative satisfaction. Several usability issues surfaced in support tickets that a structured feedback mechanism would have caught 3–4 weeks earlier. In hindsight, a fortnightly 15-minute check-in with 5 power users would have been worth more than another sprint of new features.

Case Study 02 · Arcolab · 2024
Accounts Payable Automation (AP DMS)
Eliminating manual invoice processing and digitising finance approval workflows
Product Manager · Full Ownership
The Problem
Finance teams were processing invoices entirely manually — printing, stamping, routing physical documents for approval, and re-entering data into accounting systems. A single invoice took an average of 8–12 days to clear. Errors were common, audit trails were unreliable, and month-end closings were consistently delayed by invoice backlogs.
My Role
Owned the full product lifecycle — from discovery interviews with finance controllers and AP clerks through to roadmap, BRD authoring, prototype design in Figma AI, and post-launch support coordination.
Discovery & Research

I shadowed AP clerks through their daily workflow for 3 days before writing any requirements. The most important thing I learned: the problem wasn't just slow approvals — it was that nobody knew where an invoice was in the process at any given moment. Finance heads were spending 30+ minutes per day chasing approval status across email threads and WhatsApp messages.

I also reviewed 3 months of historical invoice data (with the finance team's help) and found that 65% of delays were caused by the same 3 root causes: missing PO references, approver unavailability, and documents submitted in non-standard formats. This shaped our scope — we designed around these three failure modes specifically rather than building a generic DMS.

Key Features Built
Digital invoice intake
Structured form replacing unstructured email submission
Structured approval workflows
Multi-level approvals with SLA tracking and escalation
Document storage & audit trail
Centralised repo with full approval history
Status visibility dashboard
Eliminated "where is my invoice?" queries entirely
Key Decisions & Tradeoffs
  • AI extraction vs. structured intake form: We evaluated AI-powered invoice data extraction but deprioritised it for v1 — accuracy on non-standard invoice formats was insufficient to trust in a compliance-sensitive finance workflow. We shipped a structured intake form instead, capturing the same data reliably. AI extraction is now on the v2 roadmap with a proper validation layer.
  • ERP integration scope: Full bidirectional ERP sync was requested by the finance team but would have added significant complexity and a 6-week delay. We shipped v1 with a CSV export-import bridge, delivering 80% of the value in 30% of the time. Full integration is phased for v2.
  • Approval delegation feature: We added approver delegation (ability to reassign pending approvals when out of office) mid-sprint after discovering it was blocking our SLA targets. It wasn't in the original BRD — I made the call to add it after seeing the data on approval delays tied to approver unavailability.
Impact
~60%
Reduction in invoice processing time (est.)
100%
Digital audit trail coverage
0
Manual "where is my invoice?" queries post-launch
What I'd do differently
I underestimated how much change management a finance workflow change requires. AP clerks had used the same paper-based process for years, and even a clearly better digital system created anxiety. In hindsight, I'd have involved a "champion user" from the AP team from day one in the design process, not just UAT. Co-designing with the person most resistant to change would have caught the UX friction points earlier and created an internal advocate for adoption.

Case Study 03 · Arcolab · 2023–2025
InLumin Platform — Go-To-Market & Enterprise Rollout
Launching and scaling an enterprise SaaS platform across 3 organisations with 95% adoption
Product Manager · GTM Lead
The Challenge
Shipping a great product is half the problem. Getting a large enterprise to actually adopt it — across multiple subsidiaries, with different user personas, different IT setups, and deeply entrenched existing processes — is the other half. InLumin was a new platform with no existing user base, no internal champions, and three very different organisations to onboard simultaneously.
My Role
In addition to product ownership, I led the full go-to-market and adoption strategy — internal enablement, change management, onboarding programme design, and post-launch support operations.
GTM & Adoption Strategy

Most enterprise platform launches fail not because the product is bad but because adoption is treated as an afterthought. I treated rollout as a product problem, not a training problem.

I identified "champion users" in each organisation — typically the most process-frustrated person in each department — and worked with them 4 weeks before launch to co-configure their workflows. By the time the platform went live, these champions were already advocates. I also designed a tiered onboarding programme: a 2-hour hands-on session for power users, a 30-minute overview for occasional users, and a self-service Confluence guide for reference. The goal was to make the first experience with the platform feel like success, not confusion.

Key Decisions & Tradeoffs
  • Phased org rollout vs. simultaneous launch: We considered a big-bang launch across all 3 orgs but chose a phased rollout — Org 1 first, learnings applied, then Orgs 2 and 3. This added 3 weeks to the timeline but dramatically reduced support load and improved the experience for later orgs.
  • Configurable features vs. locked defaults: I pushed to launch with sensible locked defaults rather than exposing all configuration options immediately. Too much flexibility at launch overwhelms users. We unlocked advanced configuration in month 2, after users were comfortable with the core flow.
  • Feature requests during rollout: Multiple stakeholders raised feature requests during the rollout window. I made the deliberate decision to freeze the roadmap for the 6 weeks of active rollout — no new features, only bug fixes. Stability during adoption is more valuable than new features.
Impact
95%

What I'd do differently
I'd define success metrics for adoption more granularly. We tracked logins and workflow completions, but didn't measure depth of adoption — were users using the platform to its full capability, or just the minimum features? In hindsight, a "feature adoption funnel" tracking which features got used and when would have given us a much clearer picture of where to focus post-launch enablement.

Essay · AI in Finance Operations
Why AI in Accounts Payable Will Fail Without Trust Infrastructure

The promise of AI in AP is compelling: automated invoice extraction, anomaly detection, touchless approvals. Most vendors lead with accuracy benchmarks — "98% extraction accuracy on structured invoices." But in my experience building AP automation for enterprise finance teams, accuracy is not the bottleneck. Trust is.

Finance controllers are personally liable for errors in their books. A 2% error rate that sounds impressive in a vendor demo translates to real money and real regulatory risk. Before an AP team will let AI touch an approval workflow, they need to understand exactly when the system is confident and when it isn't — and they need an escape hatch that doesn't require IT involvement.

"The right mental model isn't AI replacing AP clerks — it's AI handling the obvious cases and surfacing the ambiguous ones for human judgment. The key product design challenge is making that handoff seamless."

The products that will win in AI-powered AP are not the ones with the best models — they're the ones that build the best confidence UI: clear exception queues, human-readable explanations for why the AI flagged something, and audit trails that satisfy both finance controllers and external auditors. That's where I'd focus product investment if building in this space today.

Essay · Enterprise Procurement
The Next Frontier in Procurement Platforms: From Workflow Automation to Decision Intelligence

Most procurement platforms today — including the one I built — automate the process of procurement: requisition, approval, PO issuance. That's necessary but not sufficient. The next wave will be about automating the decisions within procurement: which vendor to choose, when to reorder, which contracts to renegotiate.

The data is already sitting in procurement platforms — transaction history, vendor performance metrics, cycle time data, spend patterns. What's missing is a layer that surfaces actionable intelligence from that data at the moment a buyer needs to make a decision. Not a dashboard they have to remember to check, but a nudge inside the workflow itself: "This vendor has had 3 late deliveries in the past 90 days — here are 2 pre-qualified alternatives."

"Workflow automation digitises what procurement teams already do. Decision intelligence changes what they're capable of doing."

The challenge for PMs building in this space is that procurement teams are skeptical of recommendations they can't audit. Any AI-driven sourcing or vendor suggestion feature needs to show its working — not just the recommendation, but the reasoning. That transparency is what converts skeptics into power users, and power users are what drive enterprise adoption.

Sample User Story — AP Document Management System
User Story
As an Accounts Payable Clerk,
I want to submit an invoice digitally with all required fields validated at intake,
So that I can avoid rejection and resubmission delays caused by missing PO references or incorrect formats, and track the approval status in real time without chasing approvers over email.
Acceptance Criteria
  • System validates PO reference number against the PO database at submission and shows an inline error if not found
  • Mandatory fields (vendor name, invoice amount, PO reference, invoice date) must be completed before submission is allowed
  • Submitter receives a confirmation notification with a unique invoice tracking ID within 60 seconds of submission
  • Invoice status (Submitted / Pending Approval / Approved / Rejected) is visible to the submitter at all times on the dashboard
  • If approval is not actioned within the defined SLA (48 hrs), an automatic escalation notification is sent to the approver's manager
  • All approval actions are logged with timestamp and approver identity for audit trail purposes
